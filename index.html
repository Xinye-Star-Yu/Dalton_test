Deep Learning Meets Multi-Omics: Personalized Cardiovascular Risk Prediction Through Integrated Proteomic and Metabolomic Profiling

Cardiovascular disease remains a leading cause of death worldwide, and early identification of individuals at elevated risk is critical for effective prevention. While traditional clinical risk factors provide a foundation, they often fail to capture the full biological complexity underlying heart and vascular disorders. A recent study published in Nature Communications introduces a deep learning framework called CardiOmicScore that combines proteomic and metabolomic data to generate personalized risk scores for six major cardiovascular conditions, demonstrating how multi-omics integration can substantially improve prediction accuracy.

Multi-Omics Deep Learning for Cardiovascular Risk Stratification

The study leveraged data from the UK Biobank, drawing on clinical records, genomic information, and molecular measurements from large participant cohorts. The researchers designed two parallel multitask neural networks within the CardiOmicScore framework. The first network, ProNet, analyzed 2,920 circulating proteins to produce a proteomic risk score (ProScore) for each of six cardiovascular diseases. The second network, MetNet, processed 168 blood metabolites to generate a corresponding metabolomic risk score (MetScore). Both networks shared a common architecture with disease-specific output layers, allowing them to learn patterns across multiple conditions simultaneously. By training these models on metabolomics-only and proteomics-only cohorts separately, the authors ensured robust and unbiased evaluation. The six conditions examined included coronary artery disease, stroke, heart failure, atrial fibrillation, peripheral artery disease, and venous thromboembolism, covering a broad spectrum of cardiovascular pathology.

Key Findings

  • Strong proteomic prediction: ProScore demonstrated strong discriminative ability across all six cardiovascular diseases, achieving C-index values between 0.69 and 0.82.
  • Meaningful metabolomic stratification: MetScore also provided effective risk stratification, with C-index values ranging from 0.64 to 0.74 across the six conditions.
  • Enhanced combined accuracy: Combining proteomic and metabolomic scores with traditional clinical predictors improved the C-index by 0.005 to 0.102 compared to clinical data alone.
  • Long-range prediction: Risk prediction was achievable up to 15 years before disease onset, highlighting the potential for early intervention and prevention.
  • Proteomic dominance: Proteomics emerged as the dominant contributor to improved prediction, outperforming both metabolomics and polygenic risk scores when used independently.
  • Novel biomarker candidates: The framework identified key disease-associated proteins and metabolites that may serve as promising biomarkers for targeted therapeutic development.

The Role of Proteomics in Cardiovascular Prediction

Among the molecular data layers examined, proteomics provided the greatest predictive gains. ProScore consistently outperformed MetScore and polygenic risk scores across all six conditions. When combined with standard clinical variables such as age, sex, and established biomarkers, the proteomic risk score increased the C-index by 0.040 to 0.101. This finding underscores the richness of the circulating proteome as a window into cardiovascular pathophysiology. The proteins flagged by the model spanned pathways related to inflammation, coagulation, and lipid metabolism, offering potential targets for drug development and future biomarker validation studies.

Metabolomics as a Complementary Layer

While metabolomics provided more modest improvements than proteomics, it offered distinct and complementary biological information. MetScore captured metabolic signatures related to energy balance, amino acid metabolism, and lipid profiles that were not fully represented in the proteomic data. The combination of both omics layers with clinical and genetic predictors produced the highest overall predictive accuracy for most conditions studied. This complementarity highlights the value of layering multiple molecular measurement types rather than relying on a single data source, as each captures different aspects of the biological processes driving cardiovascular risk.

Implications for Precision Cardiovascular Medicine

The ability to predict cardiovascular events up to 15 years in advance opens important opportunities for early screening and targeted prevention. By identifying high-risk individuals long before symptoms appear, clinicians could implement lifestyle modifications, pharmacological interventions, or closer monitoring at a stage when prevention is most effective. The CardiOmicScore framework also demonstrates the feasibility of integrating multi-omics data into scalable computational tools suitable for clinical settings. However, the authors note that external validation in independent cohorts and across diverse populations will be necessary before such models can be adopted in routine clinical practice.

Conclusion

This study illustrates how combining deep learning with multi-omics profiling can push cardiovascular risk prediction beyond the limitations of traditional approaches. The CardiOmicScore framework demonstrates that proteomic and metabolomic data offer meaningful, complementary contributions to disease risk assessment. As large-scale biobanks and high-throughput molecular technologies become more accessible, integrating multiple biological data layers into clinical decision-making may become a practical reality, advancing the goal of truly personalized cardiovascular prevention strategies.
