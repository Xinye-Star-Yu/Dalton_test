Multi-Omics Discovery of FCGR2B as an Immunosuppressive Driver in High-Grade Serous Ovarian Cancer

Epithelial ovarian cancer encompasses five histological subtypes with distinct genetic and immunological profiles, yet the mechanisms connecting plasma proteins to immune regulation and tumor progression remain poorly understood. A recent study published in Translational Oncology employed a comprehensive multi-omics framework, integrating genome-wide association data, plasma proteomics, and immune cell trait measurements across two large cohorts to map subtype-specific protein-immune regulatory networks. Through this integrative approach, the investigators identified FCGR2B as a causal plasma protein that promotes immunosuppression and disease progression specifically in high-grade serous ovarian cancer (HGSOC).

Integrative Multi-Omics Framework for Ovarian Cancer Subtyping

The research team constructed protein-immune-cancer regulatory landscapes using a three-stage Mendelian randomization pipeline. In the first stage, subtype-stratified GWAS data from two independent EOC cohorts (comprising over 15,000 and 13,000 HGSOC cases, respectively) were cross-referenced with plasma protein quantitative trait loci derived from more than 35,000 individuals. The second stage incorporated immune cell trait data from nearly 4,000 participants to identify proteins that causally influence immune phenotypes. The third stage linked these protein-immune axes to EOC subtype risk. This approach uncovered 20 distinct subtype-specific regulatory axes. Among these, FCGR2B stood out as the most significant causal protein for HGSOC, warranting further investigation through single-cell RNA sequencing and multiplex immunofluorescence.

Key Findings

  • A three-stage Mendelian randomization framework identified 20 subtype-specific protein-immune-EOC regulatory axes across five histological subtypes of epithelial ovarian cancer.
  • FCGR2B was identified as a causal plasma protein that drives immune suppression and tumor progression specifically in HGSOC, with elevated expression strongly associated with shorter progression-free survival.
  • Single-cell analysis and multiplex immunofluorescence revealed that FCGR2B is predominantly expressed in tumor-associated macrophages and is spatially colocalized with macrophage markers CD68 and CD14.
  • FCGR2B knockdown in macrophages reduced M2-like polarization from 55.90% to 38.70% and increased phagocytic activity from 7.98% to 16.83%, indicating functional reversal of immunosuppression.
  • Transcriptomic profiling of FCGR2B-knockdown macrophages revealed altered NF-kB signaling, suggesting therapeutic vulnerability to NF-kB pathway inhibitors.

FCGR2B Localization in Tumor-Associated Macrophages

Using single-cell RNA sequencing, the investigators mapped FCGR2B expression across the HGSOC tumor microenvironment and found it predominantly enriched in tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs). Multiplex immunofluorescence confirmed spatial colocalization of FCGR2B protein with macrophage markers CD68 and CD14 within tumor tissue. Importantly, FCGR2B-positive macrophages exhibited an M2-like polarization phenotype, which is typically associated with immunosuppressive functions including dampened anti-tumor immunity and promotion of tumor growth. Survival analysis across multiple independent HGSOC cohorts consistently demonstrated that higher FCGR2B expression correlated with reduced progression-free survival, reinforcing its prognostic significance.

Functional Validation and NF-kB Signaling

To validate the functional role of FCGR2B, the team performed knockdown experiments in macrophage cell models. Silencing FCGR2B significantly shifted macrophage polarization away from the immunosuppressive M2 phenotype and enhanced phagocytic activity against tumor cells. Co-culture systems further demonstrated that FCGR2B knockdown in macrophages led to reduced proliferation and increased apoptosis of ovarian cancer cells. RNA sequencing of FCGR2B-depleted macrophages revealed substantial alterations in the NF-kB signaling pathway, a key regulator of inflammation and immune responses. These findings position FCGR2B as a functional mediator of macrophage-driven immunosuppression in the HGSOC microenvironment.

Therapeutic Prospects and Clinical Relevance

Drug-gene correlation analyses indicated that patients with high FCGR2B expression may respond favorably to MEK inhibitors. Furthermore, agents such as BI-1206, an FCGR2B-blocking monoclonal antibody currently in clinical trials, represent a targeted strategy for disrupting FCGR2B-mediated immunosuppression. The study highlights the potential for combining FCGR2B-targeted therapies with existing immunotherapy approaches to restore anti-tumor immunity in HGSOC patients who exhibit high FCGR2B expression and immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments.

Conclusion

This study presents a genetically informed, multi-omics framework that identifies FCGR2B as a key immunoregulatory protein in HGSOC. By integrating GWAS, plasma proteomics, immune cell traits, single-cell transcriptomics, and functional validation, the researchers established FCGR2B as both a prognostic biomarker and a potential therapeutic target. The work underscores the value of subtype-specific analyses and multi-layered omics integration for uncovering actionable molecular targets in complex malignancies.
